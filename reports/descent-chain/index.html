Descent chains: heuristic Lp(1/3) primality proving with Pomerance-grade verification

Analysis and prototype of Drew's proposal (2026-07-22): replace the one-shot certificate by a chain whose prime shrinks by a bit-length factor b > 1/2 per step — branch descent-chain, prototype ecpp/chain_prove.py / ecpp/vchain.py.

1 · Sanity check of the one-shot claims

Throughout, n = log₂ p, and M(n) = n log n is FFT multiplication cost.

Certificate size O(log p). Agree. The curve data is O(n) bits and the k = Θ(n/log n) primes in (n², n⁴) take O(log n) bits each.

Verification O((log p)²(log log p)²). Agree. [m]P costs O(n) group operations; establishing [m/q]P ≠ O for all k primes via the balanced product tree costs O(n) group operations per level over log k = O(log n) levels. Total O(n log n) group operations × O(M(n)) = O(n²(log n)²) — exactly one log log p factor worse than Pomerance's O(log p) multiplications. (This matches voneshot.py's own stated target, O(n²(log n)²).)

Search heuristic p1/8+o(1). Agree for the number of candidate orders; the end-to-end time of the current method is p1/4+o(1). The success condition is that the (n², n⁴]-part of a random m ≈ p exceed (p1/4+1)² ≈ √p; with u = ln p/ln n⁴ = ln p/(4 ln ln p)(1+o(1)), this has probability ρ(u/2)1+o(1) = p−1/8+o(1), so p1/8+o(1) candidates are needed — and smoothness-testing them (batched remainder trees) costs only polylog each, so that phase is indeed Õ(p1/8). The catch is producing the candidates: discriminants up to B yield only ΣD≤B Θ(1/√D) ≈ √B usable orders (the repo's own data confirms this: 10110+7 at B = 8×109 gave a pool of 39,878 ≈ 0.45√B), so B must reach p1/4+o(1) and the scan — which is Θ̃(B) — dominates at p1/4+o(1). (HD and root-finding stay Õ(p1/8): h(D) ≈ |D|1/2 ≈ p1/8.) Direct enumeration of solutions (t, v → D) is worse (Θ̃(p3/8) for the v-range that matters), so p1/8 would only become the true cost given a way to enumerate usable (D,t,v) in time proportional to their number — not currently known. In practice at 256–512 bits the o(1)'s dominate (ρ(u/2) ≈ 10−3–10−5, pools of 10³–10⁵), which is why observed runs are far friendlier than the exponent suggests — also as claimed.

2 · The descent chain

Fix b ∈ (1/2, 1). A step for a prime p is a CM-constructed E/Fp with #E = N = c·q, q prime, (p1/4+1)² < q ≤ 2b·bits(p), plus a point Q = [c]P of order q. Recurse on q; stop below 264 (deterministic Miller–Rabin range). Soundness per step is the classical Goldwasser–Kilian argument: for any prime r | p the reduction of Q has order exactly q, so q ≤ #E(Fr) ≤ (√r+1)², forcing r > √p; hence p is prime. Only q and c = N/q are needed — the factorization of c is never part of the certificate; peelability of c is purely the prover's recognition problem.

Size. Bit-lengths decay geometrically, so Σi O(nbi) = O(n)/(1−b) total — O(log p) with constant 1/(1−b) (the "O(p)" in the message I read as a typo for O(log p)). Measured: 383 B payload vs the one-shot's 126 B at 256 bits (∼3×).

Verification. Agree: O((log p)² log log p). Step i costs O(bin) group operations at M(bin) each, and Σi b2i = 1/(1−b²), so the whole chain is O(n M(n))/(1−b²) = O(n² log n) — Pomerance's order, with constant ∼1/(1−b²) (and EC-vs-Fp× group-operation overhead). The chain kills the extra log log factor precisely because each step certifies one prime and the sizes shrink geometrically, where the one-shot pays a log k-deep product tree at full size.

3 · Search complexity: subexponential, in fact Lp(1/3)

Agree that it is heuristically subexponential — and it is better than L(1/2). Per level, choose a smoothness bound y and peel cofactors by ECM (cost Ly(1/2, √2) per candidate). A candidate N ≈ p wins if its largest prime factor q lands in (√N, Nb] and the cofactor is y-smooth; winners concentrate at cofactor size σ = 1−b, so the per-candidate probability is ≈ ρ(u), u = σ ln p / ln y. With the CM scan costing Θ̃(B) = Θ̃((1/ρ)²) to produce 1/ρ candidates, balancing 2u ln u = u ln u + √(2σP/u) (P = ln p ln ln p) gives u³ln²u = 2σP, i.e. y = Lp(2/3, ·) and total heuristic cost

Lp(1/3, c(b)) with c(b) ≈ (2/3)·(18(1−b))1/3   —   c(0.85) ≈ 0.93, and c(b) → 0 as b → 1−, vs GNFS's (64/9)1/3 ≈ 1.92.

(Random curves + SEA instead of CM drop the scan-squared term and improve the constant to ≈(4.5(1−b))1/3; the ln ln-level constants are mushy either way — the robust statements are the 1/3 exponent, the (1−b)1/3 shape, and "cheaper than factoring p".) Chain length is O(log n), level sizes shrink geometrically, so the top level dominates. Letting b → 1 recovers standard ECPP (polylog-smooth cofactors, poly-time steps, but Θ(n) steps, Θ(n²) certificates, Θ̃(n³) verification); the fixed-b chain is the point on this curve that preserves Pomerance-order verification. It is also a constructive relaxation of Pomerance's 1987 very-short-proof construction (#E = 2q descent, existence only): relaxing #E = 2q to #E = c·q with c ECM-peelable and q ≤ Nb is exactly what makes the descent findable.

4 · Prototype

ecpp/chain_prove.py (prover): per level, dscan p= dump supplies (D,t,v) triples (candidate orders p+1±t); one PARI batch trial-divides all candidates to 106; surviving composite tails go through breadth-first ECM rounds (gmp-ecm 7.0.6, B1 = 2k…1M escalation, one invocation can peel several factors); the smallest in-range prime tail wins. The winner's curve comes from a polclass(−D) root; the correct twist and the point Q = [N/q]P are found and checked in Python. Every certificate is verified with vchain.py before being written. b = 0.85 default, B doubles on exhaustion, all knobs on the command line.

ecpp/vchain.py (verifier, stdlib-only): affine arithmetic over Z/p with every inversion guarded by gcd — a nontrivial gcd exhibits a factor of p; gcd 1 makes the whole computation commute with reduction mod any r | p, and the additions producing O do so via congruences that also reduce, so [q]Q = O holds mod r and Q mod r ≠ O has order exactly q. The ladder rejects any premature O (a point of prime order q cannot hit O at a proper prefix). Base case: deterministic Miller–Rabin, bases 2…37 (valid below 3.3×1024).

Validation

  • Every produced chain re-verified by vchain.py (prover refuses to emit otherwise).
  • PARI cross-check of steps with independent arithmetic: ellmul(E,Q,q) == [0] and ellmul(E,Q,q−1) != [0].
  • Tamper tests all rejected: corrupted Qx, composite base q, undersized q, broken chain link, q±ε not the point order.

6 · Follow-up: usable-discriminant density, and CM vs SEA (2026-07-22)

Why the one-shot scan is Θ̃(B) and not "one smoothness trial per discriminant". Enumerating fundamental D by |D| does visit Ω(B) discriminants up to B, but a discriminant only yields a candidate integer when 4p = t² + |D|v² is solvable — i.e. when p splits completely in the ring class field, which for a random p happens with probability 1/(2h(−|D|)) ≈ |D|−1/2. Summing, only Θ̃(√B) of the discriminants up to B consume a smoothness lottery ticket; a failed Cornacchia costs time but tests no integer. The ratio to √B is flat over four decades (the constant is a p-dependent L-value average); an Ω(B) usable-density model would have this column growing 100-fold left to right. Hence p1/8+o(1) smoothness trials force B ≈ p1/4+o(1), the scan costs Θ̃(B), and the winning |D| is ≈ p1/4 (its HD, at |D|1+o(1), matches the scan cost rather than dominating it).

CM vs random curves + SEA for the chain. Asymptotically the same √B density makes the CM scan quadratic in the number of candidates, which shifts the L(1/3) constant by the universal factor cCM/cSEA = (2/3)·41/3 ≈ 1.058 — more than a log factor, but only 6%. In practice CM is not close: one PARI ellap (SEA) order computation costs 3.3 s at 256 bits, 194 s at 512 bits, and over 50 minutes at 1024 bits, while the chain consumes hundreds of candidate orders per level and the entire dscan pool costs ∼1 s. Scanning nextprime(21023) by increasing |D| under the production ECM ladder, the smallest |D| whose p+1±t has chain shape is |D| = 2152 (h = 10, polclass ∼30 ms; q of 765 bits, cofactor cracked at the B1 = 106 level), with further winners at |D| = 2267 (B1 = 5×104), 5851 and 63944 (B1 = 1.1×104); the 38 candidate orders with smaller |D| stay composite-unresolved under the ladder, so 2152 is exact for these bounds and an upper bound for any deeper effort — the asymptotic L(1/3)-sized |D| is nowhere in sight, and class-polynomial cost never bites: even |D| ≈ 108 has h ≈ 10⁴ (seconds in classpoly). At PARI-grade SEA speeds, CM's amortized per-candidate scan cost (≈4·Ncand·10−8 s) only reaches one SEA point count once a level needs ∼109 candidates — roughly 3–5k-bit primes.

7 · Take and next steps

The descent chain is strictly better as a primality-proving scheme: same O(log p) proof size (3–4× constant), verification a log log factor faster, proving falls from p1/4+o(1) to Lp(1/3) — and in practice from "struggles past 384 bits" to "1024 bits in minutes". What it gives up is precisely the challenge's defining constraint: it is not one-shot (one certificate, one curve); the two answers sit at different points of the proof-size/aesthetics–vs–proving-effort curve rather than one subsuming the other.

  • Chain-shortening knob: the prototype takes the first (cheapest) winner; spending one extra ECM round hunting a smaller q would shorten chains toward the drop-1/2 floor at modest cost.
  • Winner selection vs class number: preferring small h(D) among winners would cut polclass time at large sizes.
  • 2048 bits: done (5.2 h on the 16-core dev box, table above); the top level is embarrassingly parallel ECM, so the 192-core spot boxes would bring it under an hour. 4096 bits is the next frontier — the top level needs ~600 bits of cofactor peeled, so expect the B1 ≥ 106 rounds to matter and a day-scale spot run.
  • C port: only worthwhile once the design settles; the Python/PARI/gmp-ecm prototype is already search-bound, not orchestration-bound.
  • Certificate tree variant: allowing 2–3 medium primes per step (certifying their product, recursing on each) trades a wider tree for easier steps; geometric decay keeps size O(n) and verification O(n² log n).
